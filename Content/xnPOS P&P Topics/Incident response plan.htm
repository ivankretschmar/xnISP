Purpose

The Incident response procedure aims to:

  • Identify accountability for responding to computer security incidents.
  • Ensure appropriate escalation.
  • Ensure effective administrative response to computer security incidents.
  • Streamline the response process.
  • Secure and protect data in order to minimise the organisational impact of a computer security incident.

Scope

This procedural document makes reference to any malicious attempt (successful or unsuccessful), to negatively impact the confidentiality or integrity of corporate (including customer) data or XPS cloud-hosted services.

Security incident types include (but are not limited to):

  1. malicious code attacks, i.e. attacks by programs such as Viruses, Trojan Horse programs, Worms, Rootkits and Scripts to gain privileges, capture passwords or modify audit logs to hide unauthorised activity;
  2. unauthorised access - includes unauthorised users logging into a legitimate account, unauthorised access to files and directories, unauthorised operation of “sniffer” devices or rogue wireless access points;
  3. disruption of services including the erasing of programs or data, mail spamming, denial of service attacks or altering system functionality;
  4. misuse involving the utilisation of computer resources for other than official purposes;
  5. espionage;
  6. hoaxes (generally an e-mail warning of a non-existent virus); and
  7. unusual events including erratic and persistent unusual system behaviour on desktops, servers or the company network, inexplicable lock out of user accounts or the existence of a strange process running and accumulating large amounts of CPU time.

Preamble

Centralised notification and control of security incident investigation is necessary to ensure that immediate attention and appropriate resources are applied to control, eliminate and determine the root cause of any event that could potentially disrupt the operation of the company or compromise company data.

Entities that have experienced a suspected or confirmed security breach must take prompt action to help prevent additional exposure of sensitive commercial or customer data.

Roles and responsibilities

To ensure incident are dealt with in an efficient manner, all members of the Computer Security Incident Response Team (CSIRT) require yearly refresher training. This training will be conducted by the Group Technical Infrastructure Director.

Computer security incident response team (CSIRT) coordinator

In the event of a security incident the IT Infrastructure Director takes the role of CSIRT Coordinator, and is charged with managing an incident. In the event that the Systems Manager is unavailable this is led by the Head of Applications Development or the CEO.

Information security

The Systems Manager reports to the Chief Executive Officer (CEO) and has responsibility for the IT security infrastructure within the company.

Computer security incident response team (CSIRT)

A CSIRT is assembled by the CSIRT Coordinator, and is drawn as appropriate, from the following groups (or their delegates):

  • Human resources
  • Finance
  • Operations
  • Public relations
  • 3rd-party specialists ( e.g. Colocation partner)

Incident response procedure

Image: Incident response process

Reporting incidents

  • All suspected computer security incidents must be reported immediately to the Technical Services Department.
  • Employees must report a suspected computer security incident according to normal practice within their business unit. This could be to their Supervisor, or to a member of the Technical Services Department.
  • Where the computer security incident involves physical security issues in addition to computer security issues, the incident must be reported to a senior Manager who will in turn alert the Technical Servies Department.

Managing incidents

Incident severity

Incidents will be classified by the CSIRT Coordinator based on the perceived impact to the company’s resources and the type of data which could have been compromised:

  • Minor: Incidents for which there are routine solutions. Sensitive information has not been exposed or accessed by unauthorised parties.
  • Medium: Incidents that do not have routine solutions but are limited in scope and consequences.
  • Severe: Incidents that involve significant personal data leakage, card holder data, compromised company data, or that impacts a significant number of users, all of which has significant consequences.

All incidents

The CSIRT Coordinator will:

  1. create an incident file;
  2. identify the scope and type of incident (including severity);
  3. notify appropriate organisations internal and external to the company (including relevant police agencies);
  4. take corrective action as described in the guidelines for securing and preserving electronic evidence;
  5. report, as needed, to the appropriate department for further action or discipline; and
  6. close the incident file.

Minor & Medium incidents

The CSIRT Coordinator will:

  1. form a CSIRT to include the relevant owner(s) of the data or issue;
  2. provide regular briefings to the CSIRT by e-mail at least once a day and more often at the outset (even if there has been “no change”); and
  3. write a closing incident report that is shared with the CSIRT.

Closing incidents

Medium & Severe incidents

A closing incident report will be prepared by the CSIRT Coordinator for medium and severe incidents.

The report should include:

  1. a chronology of the incident and summary of actions taken;
  2. ths scope of risk the company faced during the incident (e.g. number of records, degree of exposure);
  3. a description of action(s) taken to mitigate and resolve the issue;
  4. a brief explanation of the basis for key decisions;
  5. any communications sent and received;
  6. identification of internal improvements to infrastructure and systems;
  7. evaluation of whether the Incident Response procedure was followed and any recommendations for improvement; and
  8. any other recommended actions.
